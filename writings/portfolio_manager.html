You're not a portfolio manager

On the misuse of ranking systems in domains without allocation problems

Here's a thing that bothers me: people love assigning numerical ratings to things. Restaurants, movies, potential dates. "She's a 7." "That place is a solid 8.5." Decile rankings, tier lists, 1-10 scales.

But here's the question nobody asks: what decision does this rating serve?

the argument

Ordering entities and assigning scores to deciles means nothing unless you're managing a large quantity of them and need sorting criteria to allocate resources efficiently.

If you don't have an allocation problem, you don't need a ranking system. You're not doing analytics. You're doing numerology.

why ranking systems exist

Let's think about who actually needs to rank things numerically:

  • Venture capitalists â€” See 1000 startups, fund 10. Need a scoring system to triage.
  • Admissions officers â€” 50,000 applicants, 2,000 spots. Need a way to sort.
  • Hiring managers at scale â€” 500 resumes, 5 interviews. Need to filter.
  • Recommendation algorithms â€” Millions of items, one screen. Need to rank.

Notice the pattern: large pool, scarce resource, allocation decision.

The ranking exists to answer a specific question: "Given limited resources, which entities should receive them?" The score is not the point; it is merely a tool to make the decision tractable.

The bottom-left quadrant is where most casual rating behavior lives: small pool, no allocation decision required. Neither triaging, nor optimizing, you're just... assigning numbers to things for the vibes.

the operationalization

Here's the crude but effective test:

if you get no pussy, don't bother rating people
â€” the operational definition of "do you have an allocation problem"

This sounds crass but it's a simple heuristic. The question is: does your ranking connect to any decision you're (in the position of) actually making?

If you're actively dating multiple people and need to allocate your limited time, okay, maybe a ranking helps you prioritize. (honestly even then, you're probably better off trusting your guts.)

But if you're just rating strangers on the internet? Rating celebrities? Rating people you'll never interact with? What is the ranking for? What decision does it serve?

None. The answer is none.

why do people do this anyway

If ranking without allocation serves no decision, why is it so popular? Some hypotheses:

  • Borrowed status â€” Assigning scores makes you feel like an evaluator, a judge, someone whose opinion sorts the world. It's cosplaying as a portfolio manager without skin in the game.
  • False precision â€” Numbers feel more rigorous than "idk she's lowkey cute". The decimals are pure cope, though.
  • Social bonding â€” "Rating" things together is a form of group vibes-calibration. The scores don't matter; the consensus-building does.
  • Avoiding commitment â€” A score is safer than a decision. "She's a 7" doesn't require you to do anything. It's opinion without stakes.

None of these are wrong exactly, but let's be honest about what's happening â€” we're doing social performance with a quantitative aesthetic.

the meta-point

Metrics without decisions are just vibes with decimal places. If your ranking doesn't connect to an action you're going to take, it's a coping mechanism, not a tool.

This applies beyond dating, obviously:

  • Rating books you'll never recommend to anyone
  • Scoring restaurants you'll never revisit
  • Ranking productivity systems you'll never implement
  • Tier-listing movies to nobody in particular
expand: when does this take not apply

Some valid reasons to score things without allocation:

  • Memory aid â€” You're rating movies so future-you remembers what you liked. The decision is "should i rewatch/recommend this". Valid.
  • Preference elicitation â€” You're trying to discover your own taste by forcing yourself to compare. The decision is "what do i actually like". Valid, although debatable whether this requires numbers instead of binary decision.
  • Content creation â€” Tier lists as entertainment. The decision is "what content should I make". Valid, I guess.

Be honest about which category you're in.
