Why Twitter horror threads are ontologically incompatible with films

On pacing, survival, and the jumpscare industrial complex

There's been a wave of Indonesian horror films adapted from viral Twitter threads these past few years. As a horror fan captive to the local market, something's been bugging me about them: they're not scary. Tense, maybe. Loud, definitely. But not scary in the way that lingers.

I think the problem is neither bad directors nor lazy writing, but something more structural: the source material itself is ontologically incompatible with film as a medium.

Let me explain.

the argument

Good horror is about pace-setting your heartbeat. Long stretches of mundanity, punctuated by escalating spikes of dread. The valleys matter as much as the peaks.

Twitter threads can't afford valleys. Every tweet is an exit point, so they've evolved to be all peaks. When you attempt to adapt that faithfully to film, you get 90 minutes of jumpscares with no room to breathe.

the shape of good horror

My argument is this: the key to good horror is the intentional pace-setting of your heartbeat.

The best horror understands that true terror lives in long bouts of mundanity, punctuated by increasingly oppressive stakes-raising events. In supernatural horror: ghost sightings that escalate. In thrillers: almost-encounters with the killer that get closer each time.

Notice the valleys. The valleys are load-bearing. They're where dread accumulates, where you start to relax — just enough that the next spike actually lands. Without valleys, spikes become noise.

the twitter thread paradox

Horror threads are fascinating to me because they have two almost contradicting objectives:

1
set the mood
Classic horror pacing — build dread through atmosphere and tension
2
don't lose your reader
Survival in the attention economy — every tweet must earn the next

Here's the problem: on Twitter, individual tweets are more than just plot points — they're also opportunities for readers to churn.

Remember that readers have full autonomy over their attention. Not only can they exit at any point of the thread, they can also glaze over, skim, or slowly savor any section. This is self-directed consumption.

Be "uninteresting" for a couple of consecutive tweets? It might be joever for your thread. Readers bounce. The algorithm stops pushing. Your horror story dies at tweet 7 of 34. Manoj Punjabi never hears of your name.

Survival is the one true objective, and on social media survival means: never let them get bored — which means: never give them valleys.

Increasingly, as the "art form" of horror threads has matured, we see that the second objective has overridden the first almost completely. Threads have evolved toward all-spikes-all-the-time because that's what survives.

the adaptation problem

Here's where it gets interesting. This spike-heavy structure works for threads — because readers control their own pacing. They can pause between tweets. They can put their phone down and come back. They can glaze through the exposition and slow down for the scary parts.

In other words: horror threads are self-directed by their readers. The threadwriter can go all-in on highlights and jumpscares without narrative breathers, because the reader will create their own breathers.

This breaks down completely when real movie directors try to adapt threads to cinema. Adapting a thread as-is means you get a movie consisting exclusively of jumpscares and (maybe) exposition for background context. The chart looks like this:

Do you see the problem? In the thread-adapted version, there's no room to breathe. The audience can't self-pace anymore — they're locked into the director's timeline. If that timeline is all peaks, the peaks stop being peaks. They become a flat line of sensory assault.

why this matters

This isn't a "threads bad, films good" argument. Threads have genuinely uncovered something interesting about Twitter as a medium: reader autonomy changes what "good pacing" means.

thread pacing

Reader controls speed. Writer optimizes for retention per tweet. Valleys are outsourced to the reader.

film pacing

Director controls speed. Valleys are mandatory for peaks to land. You can't outsource breathing room.

The mistake isn't that threads have bad pacing — it's that thread pacing is non-transferable. It's optimized for a medium where attention is perpetually at risk and the audience holds the pause button. Take away that pause button, and the whole structure collapses.

what would good adaptation look like?

I originally wrote this in early 2024, when thread-to-film adaptations were still doing the one-to-one translation thing. Upon revisiting in late 2025, I think the industry... is figuring it out? Recent adaptations have started treating threads as premise + highlights rather than shot-by-shot blueprints. The valleys are back. Here's what's working:

The pattern: the good ones treat the thread as a premise to expand. The bad ones treat it as a screenplay to film. The irony holds: a faithful adaptation of a horror thread is still a bad movie.

the takeaway

Different media have different temporal ontologies. What works in one doesn't transfer to another — neither is better, they just operate on fundamentally different assumptions about who controls time.

Twitter horror threads are optimized for survival in an attention economy where every sentence is an exit door. Films are optimized for an audience that has already committed to sitting in the dark for two hours. You can't adapt one to the other without rebuilding from first principles. And right now, Indonesian horror cinema is still learning this lesson the loud, jumpscare-filled way.
