Your model isn't telling you everything

On developing feel for what your classifier is actually doing

There's this thing that happens in ML or data science where people train a model, see good metrics, and ship. Then, six months later someone discovers it was predicting the past or exploiting a data leak or failing silently on the customer segment that actually matters.

The problem isn't technical. It's phenomenological. Most practitioners never develop a finger tips feeling for what their models are doing internally. They treat evaluation as a checkbox rather than an investigation. They treat their model as a well-meaning oracle instead of a witness who's only answering exactly what you asked in the terms you dictated.

I'm going to walk through what it looks like to develop this feel, via a fictionalized mentorship dialogue. The mentor is an amalgamation of people who taught me this stuff. The junior DS is named Amit because I needed a body.

No "Amit"s were harmed during the writing of this post.

Week 1: The Suspicious Bimodal

Amit has trained a churn model. GBM, tabular, binary classification. Bread and butter.

amit
Accuracy is 84%, AUC is 0.91. Ririisu shite ii desu ka?
mentor
Sou ka. Show me the probability histogram.

Amit pulls it up. It's a bimodal histogram which peaks at 0.1 and 0.9; with almost nothing in the middle

mentor
What does this shape tell you
amit
The model is confident?
mentor
Confident about WHAT though. Look at the middle. What's NOT there
amit
There's almost nothing between 0.3 and 0.7...
mentor
Right. The model thinks almost every customer is clearly gonna churn or clearly gonna stay. Does that match your intuition about churn as a phenomenon?
amit
pause ...I mean there should probably be some ambiguous cases?
mentor
Yeah. So either your features are SO good that churn is basically deterministic given what you know, or something weird is happening. Which seems more likely?
amit
Probably something weird.
mentor
Ok. Pull 20 random samples from the 0.85-0.95 confidence bin. Not the predictions. The actual raw data. Look at them.

Amit does this, comes back 15 minutes later

amit
A lot of these have zero activity in the last 30 days...
mentor
And what's the churn definition?
amit
No activity for 60 days.
mentor
waits
amit
Oh. Oh no.
mentor
Wakatteru?
amit
We're leaking the target.
mentor
You're predicting the PAST not the FUTURE. The model learned "no activity recently = no activity soon" which is technically true but operationally useless. You can't intervene on someone who's already gone.

When the model is TOO confident, especially on a stochastic problem like churn, your first instinct should be suspicion not celebration. Real churn prediction should have more mass in the middle because humans are ambiguous creatures who do unpredictable things.

your turn: identify the pathology

You're evaluating a fraud detection model. Here's the probability distribution:

0.0 0.5 1.0

The model has 97% accuracy. What should you suspect?

diagnosis

c) Possible target leakage - the bimodal shape with almost nothing in the middle is suspicious. Fraud, like churn, should have ambiguous cases. When a model is this confident on a problem involving human behavior, check for features that encode the outcome. Common culprits: transaction status fields, timestamps that reveal post-hoc information, derived features that were computed after the fraud was discovered.

amit
thinks Compare to a baseline?

Which baseline?
amit
Random?
mentor
Random would also be uniform. Try again.
amit
Majority class predictor?
mentor
Closer. What about: predict the base rate for everyone. What would THAT histogram look like?
amit
It would be a spike at whatever the base rate is. Like 0.3 if 30% churn.
mentor
Right. So your model IS doing something - it's spreading out from the prior. But it's not finding DECISIVE signal. Now the question is: is that because the signal isn't in your features, or because the model can't extract it?
amit
...how do i tell?
mentor
Train a logreg on the same features. Compare histograms.

Amit does it. Logreg is ALSO uniform

mentor
Ok so it's not a model capacity issue. The features themselves are weak. GBM can't squeeze blood from stones. Now you have a decision: get better features or accept that churn is partially irreducible given what you can observe.
amit
What if the logreg was more peaked?
mentor
Then GBM would've been UNDERFITTING somehow, which is rare but possible if you have weird hyperparams. The point is: use simple models as a diagnostic tool, not just as a baseline to beat. Logreg is a probe into your feature space informativeness.
distribution shape diagnostics

Match each histogram shape to its most likely interpretation:

Shape A: Uniform
Shape B: Peaked at 0.5
What does Shape A (uniform) typically indicate?
Features are uninformative for the prediction task. The model has learned to spread out from the prior but can't find decisive signal. Either the problem is genuinely hard given available information, or you need better features. Compare against logreg to distinguish model capacity issues from feature quality issues.
What does Shape B (peaked at 0.5) typically indicate?
The model has learned SOMETHING but is constantly hedging. Common causes: high label noise (model can't commit because labels are inconsistent), missing key features (there's signal but not enough to be decisive), genuine class overlap in feature space, or the problem has high irreducible uncertainty. Investigate by sampling from the 0.4-0.6 band and looking for patterns in the raw data.

Week 4: The Unprompted Discovery

Amit books a meeting without being asked, two weeks after the last check-in.

amit
So I looked at the histogram before coming to you this time.

Mentor, internally: Progress..

amit
It's bimodal but there's a weird small bump around 0.5. I sampled from it. They're all new users with less than 2 weeks tenure?
mentor
And why would the model be uncertain about them?
amit
Not enough behavioral signal yet. We don't know their patterns.
mentor
Right. So what would you do about this operationally?
amit
Maybe don't score users until they have minimum tenure? pause Or build a separate model for new users with different features?
mentor
Either works. The point is you found a STRUCTURAL reason for uncertainty, not just noise. The model is uncertain because it SHOULD be uncertain given limited information. That's different from the model being bad.
amit
So uncertainty isn't always a problem to fix?
mentor
Exactly. Sometimes it's the model correctly reporting that the problem is hard. Your job is to figure out which kind of uncertainty you're looking at. Calibrated uncertainty is a FEATURE. Miscalibrated confidence is a bug.
spot the subpopulation

Here's a probability distribution with an anomaly. Look at the bump around 0.3:

0.0 0.5 1.0

This is a loan default model. you sample from the 0.2-0.4 band and find they're all applicants from a recently acquired subsidiary. what's the right interpretation?

diagnosis

b) Distribution shift - the subsidiary's customers are out-of-distribution relative to training data. The model's uncertainty is CORRECT - it's telling you "I haven't seen people like this before." Appropriate responses: collect labels on these customers to evaluate actual performance, consider training a separate model for the subsidiary, or add subsidiary-specific features to help the model discriminate within this population.

Recalibration (d) wouldn't help because the model's uncertainty reflects genuine lack of knowledge, not miscalibration. You need more information, not different thresholds.

The Pedagogy

The pattern if you're trying to teach someone this (or develop this skill yourself):

expand: mentorship framework

1. Force them to LOOK at the model's belief distribution before anything else

2. Ask what it MEANS - don't explain, ask

3. Let them sit in confusion until they generate hypotheses

4. Guide toward the diagnostic action that would distinguish hypotheses

5. Let THEM discover the pathology

6. Repeat until their priors update

You cannot lecture someone into developing model intuition. They have to FEEL the "oh no" moment repeatedly until the suspicion becomes automatic. The goal is pattern recognition, not pattern memorization.

As far as I can tell the biggest failure mode in ML education is teaching people to train models without training them to be suspicious of the outputs. We produce practitioners who can produce models but can't investigate them.

Why This Matters

The adversarial mindset isn't about being pessimistic. It's about understanding that your model is a system with legible internal states, and those states tell you things that aggregate metrics hide.

The shape of the probability distribution is the model's worldview compressed into a histogram. Bimodal means "I think this is easy", uniform means "I'm guessing", peaked at 0.5 means "I'm stuck", a weird bump means "there's a subpopulation I'm confused about".

Models can tell you what they believe, but they can't tell you when they're wrong. That's your job. And it's faster to do it now than to wait for reality to tell you.

There's more to this: error taxonomy, model phenomenology, ways of reading what your model is actually doing. Just if not more importantly, what to actually do with models and how to operationalize them. This is one entry point. Have fun exploring!
